Block 2 · Section C — Strategic Positioning
NAMTECH's Distinctive Positioning in India's Semiconductor Talent Ecosystem
India's semiconductor mission requires not just factories and capital — it requires a trained workforce at every layer of the value chain. NAMTECH is distinctively positioned to serve this national need through four areas of focused capability that are rare, complementary, and directly aligned with what India's semiconductor ecosystem requires over the next decade.
🏭
Backend ATMP as a Serious Academic Discipline
India's semiconductor curriculum has historically focused on frontend design — VLSI, RTL, chip architecture. Backend ATMP — wire bonding, wafer sawing, die attach, encapsulation, parametric testing — is the operational core of every approved ISM plant. NAMTECH is uniquely positioned to serve this training need with industrial-grade equipment and structured programmes.
📍
Western India Cluster — Proximity as Operational Advantage
Gujarat carries the highest concentration of ISM-approved semiconductor investment in India. NAMTECH's proximity to the Sanand, Dholera and Surat clusters — combined with a national and international partnership network spanning ArcelorMittal, Micron, Festo, Siemens, Schneider, ABB, CG Semiconductors, Kaynes and others — positions it as the natural talent anchor for this region.
👷
Technician-Scale Training at Industrial Volume
India needs 5 million semi-skilled semiconductor workers by 2030. Elite institutions serve design and research talent. NAMTECH is distinctively positioned to serve the technician and operator layer — through its admit-train-hire model, short-cycle programmes and employer-integrated curriculum — at the scale and accessibility that the manufacturing ecosystem requires.
🕸️
Hub-and-Spoke Network for Regional Talent Distribution
Delivering semiconductor manufacturing curriculum at national scale requires a hub-and-spoke architecture — standardised programmes deployed through partner institutions across regions. NAMTECH is building this network with its new campus as the hub, extending reach to ITIs and diploma colleges across Western India and beyond.

⚡ Deep Dive

Need 01 — Backend ATMP Talent at Industrial Scale

The gap: 12 plants are approved. Micron and Kaynes are already operational. CG Power commercial from 2026. TSAT Phase 1 from 2026. Each plant needs hundreds to thousands of L1-level technicians and engineers — people who can operate wire bonding machines, wafer saws, die attach equipment, encapsulation lines, and parametric test systems.

Why no one addresses it: Every IIT, NIT and private engineering college teaches frontend semiconductor — VLSI design, RTL coding, physical design. These are software-adjacent skills that can be taught in a classroom. Backend ATMP is hands-on, machine-specific, process-intensive. It requires industrial-grade equipment to teach properly. Before NAMTECH's Micron equipment arrives (post-FCRA, Feb 2027), no academic institution in India has this equipment.

NAMTECH's position: With Micron's 3 machines — Wire Bonding (KNS ICONN 1pf), Wafer Saw (DFD6362), Die Attach (Canon D02HT) — NAMTECH will be uniquely positioned to serve backend ATMP training needs with industrial-grade equipment — addressing one of the most urgent capability gaps in India's semiconductor talent ecosystem.

The PDEU learning: PDEU hired 3 dedicated engineers just to run their 3 Micron machines. Faculty alone cannot run these machines. NAMTECH must plan for dedicated technical staff from the outset.

Need 02 — Hub-and-Spoke Network to ITIs and Diploma Colleges

The gap: NITI Aayog projects 5 million semi-skilled semiconductor workers needed by 2030. The C2S programme has reached 315 universities — but these produce VLSI designers and chip architects, not shopfloor technicians. No equivalent network exists for backend manufacturing training at the ITI and diploma college level.

Why it matters: A wire bonding operator, a cleanroom technician, a yield monitoring assistant — these are the people that run ATMP plants at scale. Micron's Sanand plant and Tata's Jagiroad plant cannot operate at capacity without them. They will not come from IITs. They will come from ITIs and polytechnics — if someone builds the curriculum and trains the trainers.

NAMTECH's model: NAMTECH becomes the hub — the institution with industrial equipment, certified curriculum, and trained faculty. Partner ITIs and diploma colleges across Western India become the spokes — delivering the curriculum at scale with standardised assessment and LMS-backed tracking.

EC-13 significance: This criterion is weighted at 40 points — the highest single criterion in NAMTECH's own eligibility framework. It is weighted so heavily because it is so rare. Almost no institution has built this. Whoever does first owns the talent pipeline for a decade.

Need 03 — Short-Cycle EDP for Working Professionals

The gap: India's current semiconductor workforce stands at approximately 345,000 professionals. By 2030, it needs to reach 460,000. But the more acute problem is not headcount — it is skills mismatch. 67,000 positions are projected to remain vacant by 2030 because existing engineers and technicians lack the specific semiconductor skills those positions require.

The opportunity: As ISM plants ramp from mature nodes toward more advanced processes, their own engineers need reskilling. A process engineer at a 28nm fab will need to learn new materials, new equipment, new yield management techniques as the fab evolves. No institution is running structured Executive Development Programmes for working semiconductor professionals at scale in India.

NAMTECH's position: NAMTECH's admit-train-hire model — being developed with industry partners — is the first step. This is employer-commissioned workforce development, not just student training. Once the new campus is operational (2027), structured EDP for working professionals becomes a significant revenue stream and a strategic relationship tool with every ISM-approved company in the region.

Need 04 — L5 + L6 + Automation: The Invisible Layers

L5 — Supply Chain and Trade Compliance: Semiconductor supply chains involve 300+ chemicals and gases, equipment from 40+ countries, and export control regimes (US EAR, Wassenaar, SCOMET) that require specialist legal-technical competence. A single compliance failure on a dual-use export can result in US Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) debarment — shutting off access to all US semiconductor equipment globally. No academic institution in India trains for this. Yet every ISM plant needs L5 professionals from day one.

L6 — Utilities and Infrastructure: A 200mm fab consumes 2–4 million litres of ultra-pure water per day (18.2 MΩ·cm resistivity). A UPW system failure shuts the fab within hours. Specialty gas systems (silane, arsine, phosphine), cleanroom HVAC, chemical neutralisation — these are the most operationally critical and most invisible skills in the semiconductor industry. Nobody teaches them.

Automation and Robotics — NAMTECH's Cross-Layer Advantage: NAMTECH's existing automation and robotics capabilities — already live across its CoEs — map directly onto L3 (Automation, Robotics and Smart Manufacturing). Modern semiconductor fabs use overhead monorail AMHS systems that move 2,000+ lots per day without human contact. A few skilled automation engineers managing AMHS uptime can prevent yield losses worth ₹5–15 Cr per day. NAMTECH's automation strength is not a new capability to build — it is an existing strength that semiconductor plants urgently need. This is the pull strategy in action.

The convening dimension: EC-12 in NAMTECH's eligibility framework specifically weights institutions that have convened government-industry-academia policy dialogue. NAMTECH's work on ISM 2.0 talent pillar advocacy — bringing together the 6-layer workforce architecture, the 19-criterion eligibility framework, and the hub-and-spoke model — positions NAMTECH as a thought leader in this space, not just an applicant.

Need 06 — Western India Cluster Anchor

The geography: Gujarat has more ISM-approved semiconductor investment than any other state. Micron (Sanand), CG Power (Sanand), Kaynes (Sanand), Tata-PSMC (Dholera), Crystal Matrix (Dholera), Suchi Semicon (Surat) — all within driving distance of NAMTECH's campus. NAMTECH is uniquely positioned to serve as the talent and training anchor for this cluster — with proximity that translates directly into operational advantage for every plant in the region.

Proximity advantage: Sanand: less than 40 minutes · Dholera: less than 1 hour 10 minutes · Surat: less than 4.5 hours. Companies can send working professionals to NAMTECH for training without overnight stays. Faculty can conduct site visits and co-develop curriculum with live industrial processes. This reduces friction in every dimension of the industry-academia partnership.

Partnership ecosystem: NAMTECH's foundation spans national and international players — with ArcelorMittal as anchor organisation, alongside partnerships with Micron, Festo, Siemens, Schneider Electric, ABB, Adverb, Tata Electronics, CG Semiconductors, Kaynes ecosystem and others. This network creates a distinctive and credible foundation for building India's semiconductor talent pipeline at scale.

Looking ahead: The new NAMTECH campus in Ahmedabad (CY2028) will further strengthen the geographic anchor — purpose-built facilities at a scale that the current IIT-GN Research Park transitory space cannot match.

India's Semiconductor History — 1976 to 2021

SCL Mohali (1976) — fire in 1989 set it back. SemIndia (2007) — fell through. For four decades, every attempt stalled. What changed in 2021: COVID supply chain shock, geopolitical recognition of East Asia concentration, and a $110B import trajectory converged simultaneously.

Global Race Context

India's Semiconductor History — 1976 to 2021

India's semiconductor story did not begin with ISM. The Semiconductor Complex Limited (SCL) was established at Mohali in 1976 but lost ground after a fire in 1989. A 2007 attempt to attract foreign fabs through SemIndia fell through. For nearly four decades, every attempt stalled, collapsed or never got off the ground.

What changed in 2021 was three forces converging simultaneously: COVID-19 exposing supply chain fragility (Indian carmakers cut production by 20–25%), geopolitical recognition that 75% of global wafer fabrication capacity was concentrated in East Asia, and an economic trajectory where chip imports were heading to $110 billion by 2030.

Global Race Context

The US CHIPS and Science Act committed $52.7 billion. The EU Chips Act committed €43 billion. Japan exceeded $6 billion. India's ISM 1.0 at ₹76,000 Cr (~$9B) was significant but smaller — ISM 2.0 at ₹1.20 lakh Cr substantially closes that gap and signals a multi-generational commitment.

The ₹76,000 Cr vs ₹1.64 lakh Cr Distinction

₹76,000 Cr = Government of India's subsidy pool — maximum amount the government commits to pay (up to 50% of eligible project costs). ₹1.64 lakh Cr = Total private investment companies spend from their own balance sheets. For every ₹1 of government subsidy, ISM has unlocked ₹2.16 of private capital — a leverage ratio that validates the programme's design.

Why SPECS is Different from ISM

The Scheme for Promotion of Manufacturing of Electronic Components and Semiconductors (SPECS) targets electronic components more broadly — not just chips. Sahasra Semiconductors (Bhiwadi, Rajasthan — ₹150 Cr, inaugurated May 2026) received SPECS support, not ISM. Including it in the ISM 12 would be factually incorrect. It is noted as a complementary initiative.

SCL Mohali — Strategic Significance

SCL Mohali is India's only integrated facility producing ASICs for defence, space and aerospace. The ₹4,500 Cr modernisation over 3 years (₹900 Cr in FY26-27) is India's sovereign chip capability for strategic sectors — independent of commercial fab timelines. It also serves as the fabrication facility for C2S student chip designs (94 chips fabricated there).

ECMS — The Component Manufacturing Layer

The Electronic Components Manufacturing Scheme has been enhanced from ₹22,919 Cr to ₹40,000 Cr. This scheme supports the component and substrate ecosystem that fabs and ATMP plants depend on — reducing import dependency not just for chips but for the materials and components that go into chip packaging.

ISM 2.0 — New Scope Areas vs ISM 1.0

ISM 2.0 explicitly expands beyond ISM 1.0 to include: materials, R&D, chemicals, gases, equipment manufacturing, and skilling. This means institutions like NAMTECH — which operate in the talent and training space — now have a direct pathway into ISM 2.0 funding that did not exist under ISM 1.0.

Pilot Lines vs Commercial Production — The Distinction

CG Power inaugurated its OSAT pilot line in August 2025 — this is validation-scale production, not full commercial volumes. Micron's Phase 1 began actual commercial production (DRAM and NAND memory chips for global supply chains) in early 2026. Kaynes began commercial production in March 2026. The distinction matters because pilot lines generate learning curves but not revenue at scale.

The Tata-PSMC Significance

At ₹91,526 Cr (~$10.1B), Tata-PSMC at Dholera is India's largest single private investment in any sector. It is India's first commercial wafer fab — 28nm process node, 50,000 wafer starts per month, targeting automotive, AI, consumer electronics and 5G chips. Commercial operations targeted FY2029–30. This single project creates a massive demand for trained semiconductor engineers and technicians in Gujarat — directly in NAMTECH's catchment area.

The 6-Layer Semiconductor Workforce Architecture

NAMTECH has developed a 6-layer taxonomy covering the full semiconductor workforce — more granular than NITI Aayog's 4-layer pyramid and directly actionable for curriculum design:

  • L1 — Core Manufacturing: Process engineers, equipment engineers, yield/SPC, defect inspection, contamination control. A 1% yield improvement in a 28nm fab = ₹80–120 Cr additional revenue per year.
  • L2 — Design and Engineering: VLSI/physical design, RTL, DFT, analog/mixed-signal, layout, verification, EDA specialists, SoC architects.
  • L3 — Automation, Robotics and Smart Manufacturing: AMHS engineers, PLC/SCADA, MES integration, Industry 4.0, predictive maintenance. AMHS uptime failures can cost ₹5–15 Cr per day in yield losses.
  • L4 — AI, Digital and Data Manufacturing: AI/ML for yield prediction, digital twins, semiconductor data scientists, APC engineers. AI-enabled yield management projected to reduce fab costs by 8–12% by 2028.
  • L5 — Supply Chain and Trade: Export control/trade compliance (SCOMET/EAR/ITAR), vendor development, dual-use goods. A single compliance failure = US BIS debarment = no US semiconductor equipment globally.
  • L6 — Utilities, Sustainability and Infrastructure: Ultra-pure water (200mm fab consumes 2–4M litres/day), specialty gas handling, cleanroom HVAC, wastewater treatment. A UPW failure shuts a fab within hours.

NAMTECH's Automation Advantage

NAMTECH's existing automation and robotics programmes — already live — map directly onto L3. Modern semiconductor fabs use overhead monorail AMHS systems moving 2,000+ lots per day without human contact. NAMTECH's automation strength is not a new capability to build — it is an existing strength that semiconductor plants urgently need. This is the pull strategy in action.

The Angstrom-Scale Shift

The global semiconductor frontier has moved from nm (nanometer) to angstrom-scale. TSMC and Samsung are at 2nm commercially (2025–26). The next generation — angstrom-class — uses 2D materials like molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) instead of silicon. This is a fundamental material change, not just geometry shrink. India's approved fabs at 28nm and above are the right entry point. ISM 2.0's focus on 3nm/2nm is India's response to this shift.

India's 2D Materials Research Base

IISc Bengaluru, IIT Bombay, IIT Madras, IIT Delhi, JNCASR and TIFR have published widely on graphene, MoS2, WSe2 and heterostructures. The gap is industry linkage. The proposed India Semiconductor Research Centre (ISRC) is intended to bridge academic research to fab-relevant validation.

Pax Silica — The Geopolitical Dimension

India joined Pax Silica in 2026 — a US-led coalition to build secure silicon supply chains. Key members: USA, India, Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Australia, Israel, UK, Qatar, UAE. This is about securing access to EUV lithography (ASML, Netherlands — sole global supplier of machines costing $400M+ each) through trusted alliances — not just commercial relationships.

India's Import Trajectory

India cumulatively spent ~$150 billion on semiconductor imports between FY17 and FY25, growing at 23% CAGR. If this trend continues, annual import costs could hit $240 billion by 2035. This is the fiscal argument for ISM 2.0 that is independent of any geopolitical consideration.
